Musical Synthesizers

Synthesizers are interactive, specially designed computers that generate and modify sound through frequency synthesis. Synths are usually controlled by a keyboard, but can also be controlled by a digital (digital synth) or tangible soundboard (analog synth). They reached their peak popularity in the 70's and 80's, but their musical influence both preceeds and suceeds these time periods.


  • The earliest and most primitve version of the synth was called the theremin (originally called the "etherphone"). Invented in 1922 by Leon Theremin, the theremin produces a singular eerie, wailing sound. It is a relatively small but complicated synth that is played, oddly enough, without any actual touching. In simplest terms, the theremin relies on the vibration frequency of your hands to control the pitch and volume of the sound that is produced.
    Price: $400-$1,00

  • The Ondes Martenot synth, invented in 1928 by Maurice Martenot, is a bit more advanced than the theremin but not yet digital. Unlike the theremin, the Ondes Martenot synth relies on hands-on control. The synth consists of a keyboard/piano, a pitch thread with an attched ring, and a dynamics operator that slid out from an attched drawer. The idea of the synth was to create an instrument that has a quickly shifted between, wide pitch variable. To acheive this pitch variable, the player would insert their ring finger into the the metal ring and slide it across the attatched thread. The sound dynamics were created by pressing down on a glass bar in the dynamics operator. The original Ondes Martenot produced its sound via sine waves, but its later versions included the variety of waves (pulse,triangle, and square) that would become a foundational part of later synths.
    Price: Unknown, though an original model could be very expensive.


    • Source: wikipedia.com, (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ondes_Martenot)

It was intened to emulate the same quick shifted pitch sound as the theremin, but be able to be played on a keyboard.   • The Moog synth and its miniature counterpart, the Minimoog, carry the line of synth development into more digitally progressive bounds. The Moog Modular was the first analog synth to be comerically manufactured, as it was the first offical musical synthesizer that acted as more than a prototype or a trial. The science of artificial sound synthesis had been cultivated and perfected by the experimental synths that came before the Moog, so in a way this synth was the magnum opus of its time in development. Its inventor, Bob Moog, included many of his patented artificial sound synthesis devices in it, making the synth one of the most efficient and intricate at the time. The Minimoog was the smaller, more consice "child" of the Moog Modular that made its revolutionary charcateristics more widely acessible.
    Price: Models can range from $400-$3,000


    • Source: Sean O'Kane, theverge.com (https://www.theverge.com/2015/1/19/7853651/moog-modular-synthesizers-comeback)




  • The Yamaha DX7, released in 1983 was the best selling and most efficient synth of its time, although there was some controversy surrounding it. This synth came with a multitude of pre-programmed sounds that made it a good choice for live performances, but some disliked this feature and felt it lacked dynamicy and character. Many of those who dislike the DX7 believe it is to blame for the transition from creativity to genericy in electronic music.
    Price: $600-$1,500


    • Source: spectralplex.com, (https://spectralplex.com/yamaha-dx7-synthesizer/)




  • The Roland, Microkorg, and Eurorack synths are the leading synths in today's realm of music production. The most revolutionary of the modern synth lineup are undoubtedly the Roland TR 808 and the Roland TR 303. Both of these synths are the foundation of dance, hip hop, and rap as we are familiar with it. The TR 303 model was especially important because of the new genre of electronic music it birthed, although before its rise it popularity it was deemed to have too complicated an interface. In 1987, a chicago-based group named Phuture compiled a 12 minute composition named "Acid Tracks". The newfound use of the TR 303's resonance capabilities birthed a brand new genre of electronic music: Acid House.
    Price: $500-$1500
